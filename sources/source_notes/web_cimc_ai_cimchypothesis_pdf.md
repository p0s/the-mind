## One-paragraph summary
Position paper proposing a functional/computational account of consciousness intended to be testable in machine simulations. It separates phenomenology (second-order perception; present/presence; optionally a self-in-a-world stance) from a proposed functional role (coherence-maximization over competing models; attention as an orchestration mechanism; and an early developmental “genesis” role for building complex reality models). It argues that there can be no purely behavioral “Turing test” for consciousness, and sketches an “extended” machine-consciousness program: recreate self-organizing learning conditions on digital substrates, set tasks that require agency, and search for architectures/training regimes whose internal organization plausibly instantiates the proposed functional/phenomenological package.

## Candidate claims to add to claim ledger
- Consciousness can be characterized by both phenomenology and functional role; it is treated as a specific dynamic representation with an indispensable role in cognition.
- Phenomenology of consciousness is described as second-order perception, including the experience of present/presence, and (optionally) an observing self in a world.
- Consciousness can be modeled functionally as a coherence-maximizing operation over simultaneously active partial models, with attention drawn to contradictions/disharmonies.
- Neural correlates of consciousness are useful descriptive targets, but correlational identification alone does not provide an explanatory/operational theory.
- There is no purely behavioral test (“Turing Test”) for consciousness; a test must incorporate constraints on internal organization/structure.
- Realness is treated as a representational feature that marks content as “currently the case”; “phenomenal reality” is sensory content “currently being confirmed.”
- The psyche is described as the representational structure combining a personal self with motivational “strings,” within a mind-model of self, interests, and world.
- A machine-consciousness research program can be framed as searching for analogous learning algorithms by recreating self-organization conditions on digital substrates and training for agency-requiring tasks.
